Electrical Code Requirements for Bathrooms

The bathroom is one of the most critical areas in a home, requiring specific electrical code requirements to ensure safety and functionality. As a DIY enthusiast embarking on a bathroom renovation, it is essential to understand and adhere to these requirements to avoid costly rework, electrical hazards, or even code enforcement issues.

Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter (GFCI) Protection

  • All 125-volt, single-phase, 15- and 20-amp receptacles installed in bathrooms, including those above countertops, sinks, and bathtubs, must be GFCI-protected.
  • GFCIs must be located within sight of the receptacle or be accessible to the user.
  • Metal GFCI receptacles are not required; however, they are recommended to provide additional protection against electrical shock.

Branch Circuit Requirements

Bathrooms must have a dedicated branch circuit for lighting and receptacles. The circuit must be rated at 20 amps and have two 20-amp breakers in the main electrical panel.

  • Bathroom lighting must be connected to a dedicated 20-amp circuit, unless the lighting is controlled by a single-pole switch.
  • Heated mirrors, towel warmers, and heated floors must be connected to a dedicated 20-amp circuit.
  • The 20-amp circuit must have a 20-amp breaker and a separate 20-amp outlet to prevent overloading.

Receptacle Requirements

  • Receptacles installed in bathrooms must be tamper-resistant (TR) receptacles, as required by the National Electric Code (NEC).
  • Receptacles in bathrooms must be grounded and have a grounding clip or ring.
  • Receptacles must be placed 36 inches or more from the water's edge in bathtubs or showers and at least 12 inches above the floor in areas subject to water splashing.

Lighting Requirements

Bathroom lighting must be provided to ensure adequate illumination for the area. This typically includes three-way switching for the main lighting and a separate switch for overhead ventilation.

  • Main lighting must be installed in a way that prevents glare and reflections.
  • Recessed lighting must be installed at least 6 inches outside the bathtub or shower area.
  • Lighting must be installed in a way that prevents electrical shock, such as not having live parts exposed to water.

Additional Requirements

Additional bathroom electrical code requirements include:

  • Permit required for all electrical work, including relocations and installations.
  • Electrical panels and circuit breakers must be located outside the bathroom or in a dedicated electrical room.
  • Smoke and carbon-monoxide alarms must be installed on every level of the home.

These bathroom electrical code requirements can change over time, so it's essential to verify them with your local electrical code authorities before embarking on your DIY bathroom renovation project. By understanding and following these requirements, you can ensure your bathroom is safe, functional, and code-compliant.
